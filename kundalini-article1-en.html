NOT MAGIC — BIOLOGY

What happens to body and brain during Kundalini activation?

← About Kundalini

When we hear the word "Kundalini", the imagination often conjures images of Indian yogis, mystical metaphors about a coiled serpent and complex esoteric rituals. But what if we look at this process through the lens of modern science? It turns out that Kundalini awakening is not magic at all — it is a fully measurable bioelectric and neurophysiological process.

Where does the "sleeping serpent" live?

Ancient texts say Kundalini sleeps at the base of the spine. Modern anatomy locates the coccygeal gland there — a unique neuroendocrine node. Its activation triggers a cascade of reactions: the release of noradrenaline and nitric oxide shifts the nervous system into a state of heightened conductivity. Cerebrospinal fluid becomes superconductive, and the person physically experiences the very flow that the ancients described as "the serpent rising".

The brain on Kundalini frequency

EEG and MRI research shows remarkable changes during activation sessions. The prefrontal cortex, brainstem and limbic system all activate. Brain rhythms shift: alpha, theta and gamma waves intensify. The corpus callosum begins synchronising both hemispheres — the left (logical) and right (intuitive) sides of the brain start working as one. This is precisely what gives the person that feeling of extraordinary clarity, expansion and inner light.

The alchemy of hormones

When energy rises naturally, without forcing the body, a sustained hormonal reorganisation begins. The body starts producing natural endorphins, serotonin and oxytocin in a harmonious rhythm. This is why people who have experienced activation report not just a brief ecstasy, but a deep, long-term reduction in stress and a return of their taste for life.

Kundalini is our baseline frequency. In awakening it, we are not introducing anything foreign to the body. We are simply switching on the perfect biological system that was given to us by right of birth.
